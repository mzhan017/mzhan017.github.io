# [Random Talk] The Inevitability of Information Lag?
A few days ago I wrote this piece: [Random Talk] Can Even This Be Open-Sourced? It’s about an open-source website that compiles war situation summaries. I gave it a read, and it’s actually pretty well-done.
## Summary of the reference in following article
Then I checked the references cited in its articles—for instance, one summary drew on more than a hundred online sources (https://understandingwar.org/research/middle-east/iran-update-february-26-2026/). I wanted to see which of these references I could access (if I could read them myself, I’d get information a bit faster and earlier; maybe I could even get third or fourth-hand news?). 
### Data Show
But the results were striking, and here are the numbers:
Column G is the number of websites; Column H is the number of source materials cited in the articles. Since content from some websites was cited multiple times, the red figure is higher than the blue one. As you can see, only about 20% of the English-language websites are accessible. This number is truly eye-opening—by default, others have 80% more information than I do; how can one compete with that?

## Conclude
Beneath another piece [Random Talk] Trends in the US-Israel-Iran War, someone even questioned that the information was too outdated. And yes, there’s a reason for the lag/obsolescence.
Judging from these figures, information lag is an inevitable outcome. Timely access to information simply isn’t possible.
